Friday was the coldest January 5 in Toronto history: -22 Celcius (-7.6 Fahrenheit).

It was cold! It really was. I stayed in and worked from home because the thought of going out there was just not appealing.

Then I thought about it a little more. I grew up in Calgary, Alberta, which is also‚Ä¶ very cold. Earlier this week the zoo had to bring their penguins in because it was too cold üêß. But Calgary is also famous for chinooks, which are warm winds that can drastically change temperature ‚Äì having days go from -20 C to +10 C for a few hours was not uncommon!

I moved to Vancouver for grad school and lived there for a few years. Vancouver is, of course, famous for mild temperatures and grey skies. I went to school on a mountain and the campus would close down when it snowed because busses couldn‚Äôt get up there ‚Äì but that was maybe once a year. Some years I lived there it didn‚Äôt snow at all, though people do love talking about how that wet cold really ‚Äúgets in your bones‚Äù. Right now, Vancouver is experiencing its longest cold snap in over 30 years: average temperatures below +5 C for 33 days. I did have to correct myself because I initially wrote below -5 C‚Ä¶ nope, +5 C. Boohoo ‚ùÑÔ∏è

Toronto is just‚Ä¶ cold. No chinooks, no +5 Celcius winter temperatures.

I thought it would be interesting to compare the weather in the three cities I‚Äôve lived in (Calgary, Alberta; Vancouver, British Columbia; Toronto, Ontario) over the last 5ish years, since I started moving around. Did I make the right call? Should I have stayed in Calgary and continued to be cold forever? Did I move from Vancouver at a good time, since it‚Äôs colder there now than ever before (well, in my lifetime)?

Of course, moving around has a lot more to do with the weather. But this is just fun.

I want to compare the average daily temperatures for the last year-ish to to four years prior. For example, I want to compare the temperature on January 5, 2018, to the January 5 temperatures from 2013 to 2017. I want to compare temperatures in summer 2017 to temperatures in summers 2013 through 2016.

This is where things are going to get a little hacky. If you know of a better way to do this, please let me know!

I‚Äôm going to extract the month-day portion from each date and create two new data frames ‚Äì one for previous years, and one for this year. I‚Äôm counting the first few days of 2017 in with previous years, and everything else in 2017 as part of this year.

city_weather_summary <- city_weather_summary %>%
  mutate(month_day = strftime(date, format = "%m-%d"))

city_weather_summary_previous_years <- city_weather_summary %>%
  filter(date < "2017-01-06") %>%
  group_by(city, month_day) %>%
  summarise(temp_c = mean(mean_temp_c, na.rm = TRUE),
            timeframe = "previous years' average")

city_weather_summary_this_year <- city_weather_summary %>%
  filter(date >= "2017-01-06") %>%
  select(city, month_day, temp_c = mean_temp_c) %>%
  mutate(timeframe = "this year")

Then I‚Äôm putting them together into one data frame.

city_weather_summary_combined <- city_weather_summary_previous_years %>%
  bind_rows(city_weather_summary_this_year) %>%
  arrange(city, month_day) %>%
  mutate(temp_c = round(temp_c, 1))

Ok, this is where it gets totally hacky. In order to plot the data over time, I need to ‚Äúreconstruct‚Äù an actual date, since you can see that the month_day variable is a character, not any sort of date. I‚Äôm converting every date to be in 2017, then converting dates that are in early January to be 2018 dates. Again, this is because I lumped dates in early 2017 with the ‚Äúprevious years‚Äù, since ‚Äúthis year‚Äôs‚Äù January dates are early 2018. I‚Äôm confusing myself too, I know. I should have done this analysis on December 31 üòã

city_weather_summary_combined <- city_weather_summary_combined %>%
  mutate(date = ymd(paste0("2017-", month_day)),
         date = if_else(date > "2017-01-06", date, date + years(1)))

The Calgary colds are cold, that‚Äôs for sure. We see daily temperatures as low as -27.7 C (!), whereas Toronto only gets to -16.2 and Vancouver gets to‚Ä¶ -3.2. I‚Äôm not sour about moving, I promise ‚Äì this analysis fails to take into account the impact of those grey, grey skies ‚òÅÔ∏è.

While Toronto didn‚Äôt have a super hot summer this year, we did get some nice warm weather in September and October ‚Äì we saw average daily temperatures as high as 25 C in those months, while Calgary saw average daily temperatures as low as -1 C. I remember a lot of white halloweens as a child!

As I mentioned, Toronto didn‚Äôt have a very hot summer this year. I was told to anticipate the worst of the worst: sticky, sticky days, with high humidity. Was this summer colder than previous ones?

I know I‚Äôm comparing 1 year‚Äôs data to the average of 4 previous years‚Äô, but bear with me. We see that this year‚Äôs August has many days colder than or similar to the previous years‚Äô average, while mid-to-late September was hotter than the past few years. Of course, I‚Äôm not looking at humidity ‚Äì there were a few very sticky days ‚Äì but it wasn‚Äôt as unreasonably hot as everyone had warned (and, honestly, as I had hoped üòé).

What about Vancouver? Did I make a good call avoiding this year‚Äôs winter?

Some parts of November seemed to be warm but December and January are looking pretty cold so far! I‚Äôve heard lots of reports of friends in Vancouver and Victoria dealing with colds they‚Äôve never seen.

I can‚Äôt pretend that it‚Äôs not cold in Toronto, maybe even colder than previous years. Global climate change is real (duh) and none of this analysis intends to explore that at all. But I‚Äôm thankful that my first Toronto summer wasn‚Äôt too scorching, that we got a nice warm fall, and that I have a warm home to stay in when it‚Äôs just too cold to bear outside. Many are not so fortunate!
